IPO

Initial Public Offer | NSE | BSE

An IPO is an opportunity to become a passive part owner of a business at its point of entry into public markets. The decision involves more variables than investing in an already listed security — the company's prospects have not yet been validated by the market. The likely gains that may occur on listing are not the only measure. Participating in an IPO requires the same seriousness as any equity investment — an assessment of the business, not just the opportunity.

We facilitate IPO applications through BSE and NSE platforms for eligible investors. The process, timeline, and allotment mechanics are routinely shared with investors showing interest.

Unlisted Shares

Pre-IPO | Private Placement

Unlisted shares represent ownership in a company before it enters public markets. Such transactions typically arise when an entrepreneur seeks to raise capital or when early investors choose to exit — for reasons not always publicly available. The range of outcomes is wide — phenomenal gains or catastrophic losses — driven by factors such as governance, competitive dynamics, and technological shifts that continue to evolve long after the investment is made. This is a high risk, high gain asset class.

Lower liquidity, limited price discovery, and a longer holding horizon are inherent characteristics of this asset class, not exceptions to be managed around.

This is not a product for every investor. For those who understand what they are acquiring and on what terms, we may facilitate transactions.

Futures & Options

NSE

Futures and options are instruments of risk management, designed to hedge existing exposures against exceptional market volatility. That is their original and legitimate purpose.

Warren Buffett, writing in Berkshire Hathaway's 2002 annual letter, described derivatives as "financial weapons of mass destruction, carrying dangers that, while now latent, are potentially lethal." The warning was directed at the financial system. It applies with equal force to the individual investor who enters this segment without full understanding of what they are holding.

Peter F. Drucker, in Management Challenges for the 21st Century (1999), observed that the world's largest financial institutions — American, British, Japanese, German, French, Swiss — had increasingly resorted to "trading for their own account," that is, to outright speculation. This, he wrote, "as centuries of financial history teach (beginning with the Medici in 15th-century Europe), has only one — but an absolutely certain — outcome: catastrophic losses." What Drucker described at the institutional level, retail investors in the F&O segment are replicating at the individual level — with identical results.

Published research by SEBI on the equity F&O segment reveals that over 9 out of 10 individual traders incur losses — with aggregate retail losses exceeding Rs. 1.8 lakh crore over just three years. Viewed differently, every rupee lost by the nine is going somewhere. It is going to the tenth participant — the well-capitalised, technologically equipped proprietary desks, algorithmic traders, and institutional players who enter this segment with information advantages and processing speeds that the retail investor cannot match and was never meant to compete with.

They understand retail behaviour with precision. They know that the retail investor carries two simultaneous pressures: the desire to grow small savings meaningfully, and the anxiety of not being able to afford a serious loss. That combination makes retail participation predictable. Predictable participants are, by definition, profitable counterparties for those on the other side.

The techniques that bring retail investors to this table are not accidental. Zero and nominal brokerage offers make the cost of entry feel trivial. Free tips and calls create an illusion of guided certainty. Within many broking organisations, compensation structures reward employees on the basis of turnover generated. This creates a persistent internal pressure to encourage more trading, which compounds the external luring mechanisms already at work on the retail investor. Financial media amplifies winners and quietly buries losses. Peer accounts of exceptional gains do the rest. The retail investor does not feel pushed into this segment. They feel invited. That distinction matters — because invited participants rarely examine the terms of the invitation.

The instrument itself demands respect. Unlike equities, a derivatives position carries daily cash obligations regardless of your intention to hold. A position that moves against you does not wait. Losses can exceed the amount originally committed. These are not warnings in fine print. They are the lived experience of many retail participants.

Clients who understand the instrument and appreciate the full implications of participating in this segment are welcome to trade futures and options on NSE through us.
